# GPU VS TPU VS NPU: What's the Difference?

Release Time: Dec 08, 2025

Section

As is well known, Artificial Intelligence (AI) is driving technological innovation, particularly in the realm of large-scale data processing. Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Learning (DL) algorithms, designed to solve complex problems and learn autonomously, are continuously pushing the boundaries of computing capabilities.

As the problems requiring computer processing become increasingly complex, the number of running processes has exploded. This growth has spawned specialized processors and a plethora of new terminology.

In addition to the familiar **Central Processing Unit** (CPU), the **Neural Processing Unit** (NPU), **Graphics Processing Unit** (GPU), and Tensor Processing Unit (TPU) have joined the processor lineup.

Next, we will break down the working principles of these specialized processors and the differences between them. If you are interested, stay tuned for an IT history lesson - I will delve deeper into the more technical concepts behind the fusion of hardware and software development over the past century.

![GPU VS TPU VS NPU](/files/uploads/editor/b/2025120816414911.webp)

## **Central Processing Unit (CPU): The Veteran of Calculation**

Think of the CPU as the general of the computer. It consists primarily of an Arithmetic Logic Unit (ALU) and a Control Unit. The ALU is responsible for executing arithmetic operations (addition, subtraction, etc.) and logical operations (AND, OR, NOT, etc.); the Control Unit regulates the ALU, memory, and input/output functions, instructing them on how to respond to the program just read from storage.

The most intuitive way to understand the CPU's mechanism is to trace its input/output flow: the CPU receives a request (input), accesses computer memory to retrieve task instructions, delegates execution to its own ALU or other specialized processors, and subsequently, the Control Unit consolidates all data to finally take a unified action (output).

![Circuit diagram of a 1970 ALU](/files/uploads/editor/b/202512081644251.webp)

\[Circuit diagram of a 1970 ALU\]

But more importantly, the following diagram clearly illustrates the operating logic of a CPU:

![CPU operation logic](/files/uploads/editor/b/20251208164448cpu-operation-logic.webp)

With the evolution from single-core to multi-core processors, CPU performance has become increasingly powerful. The core lies in multiple Arithmetic Logic Units (ALUs) executing tasks collaboratively, scheduled by the CPU's Control Unit to achieve parallel processing. This architecture allows the CPU to work efficiently with specialized AI processors like GPUs.

## **The Rise of Specialized Processors**

When a computer receives a task, the processor first needs to communicate with memory—including Program Memory (ROM) for fixed tasks like booting, and Data Memory (RAM) for frequently changing operations like loading applications, editing documents, or browsing the web. The bus connecting these components acts as the data transmission channel, but it can only access one memory type at a time.

In the past, processors ran slower than memory access, but as processor technology evolved, this situation reversed. Today, when processing massive amounts of data, the CPU is often forced to wait for memory access due to bus transmission congestion. This not only lowers efficiency but also leads to a sharp increase in energy consumption. The computing world calls this phenomenon the "Von Neumann bottleneck." With the emergence of complex tasks like AI, breaking this bottleneck is imminent.

One solution is to develop chips optimized for specific tasks. Specialized chips can effectively relieve the processing pressure placed on the CPU by machine learning algorithms. In the race to build the best AI processor, giants like Google, IBM, Microsoft, Nvidia, and Huawei are achieving more complex logic queries through specialized processors, each with its own unique technical path. Let's specifically analyze the characteristics of GPUs, TPUs, and NPUs:

## **Graphics Processing Unit (GPU)**

The GPU was originally a professional graphics processor, often mistaken for a graphics card containing more hardware. Its design intent was to support massive parallel processing, working in tandem with the CPU (either fully integrated into the motherboard or as independent hardware to handle high-load tasks), though the heat generation caused by high energy consumption cannot be ignored.

GPUs were long applied in the gaming sector until the early 21st century, when Nvidia pushed for a shift toward general-purpose computing. Beyond chip design, Nvidia launched the CUDA proprietary platform, allowing programmers to directly access the GPU's virtual instruction set and parallel computing units. This means calculation kernels (clusters of processors working together) can be built to specialize in specific tasks without overly occupying other resources. The diagram below visually demonstrates its workflow:

![GPU workflow](/files/uploads/editor/b/20251208164534gpu-workflow.webp)

\[Image: GPU workflow\]

This enables GPUs to be widely used in machine learning tasks, with the advantage of leveraging an existing, mature ecosystem. In other words: when looking for a solution, the winning proposal is not always the one with purely the best performance. If a technology requires a complete change in user behavior or requires everyone to relearn skills, it often faces resistance in adoption. Therefore, the GPU's high compatibility with existing systems and programming languages significantly promoted its popularity. Although not quite "plug-and-play," the core advantages are obvious.

With technological development, open-source GPU platforms supported by industry giants (including Nvidia) have emerged, the largest being OpenCL. Furthermore, Tensor Cores have been added (the principles of which will be explained later in this article).

## **Tensor Processing Unit (TPU)**

The good news: this term simply refers to [Google's proprietary AI processor](https://www.lovechip.com/blog/google-tpu-vs-nvidia-gpu-a-comprehensive-technical-comparison). Google has been using them in its own data centers since 2015, released them to the public in 2016, and now has several commercial models available. They operate based on ASICs (application-specific integrated circuits, detailed later) and Google's TensorFlow software.

Compared to GPUs, the special feature of the TPU is its adoption of slightly lower computational precision, which allows it to handle different types of workloads more flexibly. Google's explanation is particularly insightful:

"If it is raining outside, you don't need to know the exact number of raindrops per second—you just need to know how heavy the rain is. Similarly, neural network predictions typically do not require the precision of 32-bit or even 16-bit floating-point numbers. Through optimization, using 8-bit integers to calculate neural network predictions can still maintain appropriate accuracy." (Excerpt from Google Cloud Blog)

GPUs, originally designed for graphics processing and rendering, rely on the precise relationship between pixels. If point precision is insufficient, vector calculations generate amplified errors, and the final effect would be like the difference in image quality between "Spyro the Dragon on PS2" and "Spyro the Dragon on PS4."

Another key design difference from CPUs and GPUs is that the TPU uses a systolic array architecture. This architecture builds a processor network where each node executes part of the calculation task and then passes the result to the next node until the entire processing flow is complete. The nodes are typically fixed and identical, but the programs running between nodes can be customized. This structure is called a Data Processing Unit (DPU).

## **Neural Processing Unit (NPU)**

"NPU" is sometimes used as a blanket term for all specialized AI processors, but it more specifically refers to processors designed for mobile devices.

The NPU contains all the components needed to complete AI processing, operating based on the principle of synaptic weights. This concept, borrowed from biology, describes the connection strength between two neurons: when two neurons frequently transmit information, the connection between them physically strengthens, making energy transmission more efficient—this is the scientific principle behind habit formation. Many neural networks simulate this mechanism.

The learning process of AI algorithms is embodied here: the system continuously tracks potential possibilities and strengthens the connection weights of relevant nodes. This is significant for power control. While parallel processing can execute multiple tasks simultaneously, it falls short in coordinating task completion, especially when architecture expansion leads to scattered processing units.

## **Summary of Core Differences**

There is a lot of information here; these are the key takeaways:

● **Functional Positioning:** GPUs were originally developed for graphics rendering, while TPUs and NPUs are built specifically for AI/machine learning workloads.

● **Parallel Processing:** GPUs are designed for parallel processing, suitable for training complex neural networks. TPUs specialize this feature, focusing on tensor operations to achieve higher speed and energy efficiency.

● **Level of Customization:** TPUs and NPUs are highly customized for AI tasks, whereas GPUs provide a general-purpose solution suitable for various computing tasks.

● **Application Scenarios:** GPUs are commonly used in data centers and workstations for AI research and training; TPUs are widely applied in Google Cloud infrastructure; NPUs are ubiquitous in AI-enabled devices like smartphones and IoT devices.

● **Availability:** GPUs are widely provided by multiple manufacturers and are accessible to researchers, developers, and enthusiasts; TPUs are exclusive resources of Google Cloud services; NPUs are integrated into specific devices.

## **Do These Differences Matter?**

The definitions of different processors may seem similar at first glance. Multi-core processors integrate multiple ALUs under a central control unit; GPUs integrate even more ALUs under a specialized processor; TPUs integrate multiple computing nodes under a DPU similar to a CPU.

Ultimately, while there are subtle differences in design choices between processors, their true impact is seen at the level of large-scale application rather than at the consumer level. Specialized processors can handle large datasets more efficiently, meaning faster processing with less electricity (though as AI tools are used more frequently, total power consumption may rise).

Equally important, these terms themselves belong to a rapidly evolving emerging field. Google's TPU was released in 2015, just 10 years ago. Understanding these physical realities, the challenges we had to overcome, and their solutions can help us utilize these tools more efficiently—and create even cooler things in the future.

## **A Brief History of Hardware Development**

This leads to our history lesson. To understand today's topic more deeply, one must know a little about how computers are physically built. The most basic language of a computer is binary code, represented by sequences of 0s and 1s. These values correspond to the on/off state of circuits: current cannot pass when the circuit is closed (off), and can pass when open (on). [Transistors](https://www.lovechip.com/category/transistors) are used to regulate current, generate electrical signals, and act as switches or gates. Connecting a massive number of transistors with circuits constitutes an Integrated Circuit (IC) chip.

The combination patterns of transistor on/off states can be read by the computer. As the number of transistors increases, the range of numbers that can be represented by binary code grows larger. This influenced our basic computational foundation for measuring bits and bytes: eight transistors store one byte of data (each transistor has two states, and the combination of all possibilities (2^8) creates 256 on/off gate (bit) combinations). Therefore, 8 bits = 1 byte, which can represent any number between 0 and 255.

The shrinking size of transistors and the increase in transistor density on a single chip have driven improvements in computing power, speed, and energy efficiency. This is mainly due to breakthroughs in semiconductor material purification technology, the use of precision processes like chemical etching, and improvements in cleanroom technology. It all started with the birth of the integrated circuit chip.

Around 1958, multiple scientists conquered different technical difficulties almost simultaneously, collectively leading to the invention of the integrated circuit. Jack Kilby of Texas Instruments developed the hybrid integrated circuit, measuring about 7/16 × 1/16 inches (11.1 × 1.6 mm). Later, Intel co-founder Robert Noyce pioneered the monolithic integrated circuit (integrating all circuits onto the same chip), with similar dimensions.

Notably, the initial chips could only hold about 60 transistors. Today's microchips can etch billions of transistors onto an even smaller size. The image below shows a disassembled physical integrated circuit:

## **What is an FPGA?**

Everything described above refers to fixed physical units on a chip, but chip performance is also influenced by software. Software defines the logic and instructions for how all components work together. Therefore, when manufacturing chips, there are two choices: either customize specialized chips for specific software or adopt "blank chips" that can be reconfigured at any time.

The first type is called an Application-Specific Integrated Circuit (ASIC). But like all proprietary manufacturing processes, it must be mass-produced to be profitable, and the production cycle is long. CPUs and GPUs typically operate based on this type of hard-wired chip.

Reconfigurable chips are called [Field-Programmable Gate Arrays](https://www.lovechip.com/blog/fpga-chip-design-fundamentals-design-flow-and-applications) (FPGA). They possess high flexibility, providing developers with various standard interfaces. This is extremely valuable for AI applications (especially deep learning algorithms)—when technology iterates rapidly, FPGAs can achieve multi-functionality through reprogramming on the same chip, allowing developers to quickly complete testing, iteration, and market launch. The most prominent manifestation of their flexibility is reprogrammable input/output interfaces, thereby reducing latency and breaking bottlenecks. For this reason, the industry often compares the entire ASIC processor category (CPU/GPU/NPU/TPU) with FPGA performance, which has also given rise to hybrid solutions.

At the same time, software technology has enabled the networking of multiple processing cores, increasing calculation speed through load distribution. All these advances have jointly spawned specialized chips that meet the massive demands of AI.

## AI Chips: GPU, TPU, and NPU Frequently Asked Questions

### **1\. What bottleneck does the CPU encounter in AI task processing that led to the emergence of specialized processors?**

Answer: As computing problems become increasingly complex and data volume explodes, the Central Processing Unit (CPU) runs into the well-known "Von Neumann bottleneck." This occurs because the CPU's execution speed has become faster than its memory access speed when dealing with massive datasets. Data transmission between the processor and memory must pass through a bus, often leading to congestion, forcing the CPU to wait for memory access. This waiting not only reduces efficiency but also sharply increases energy consumption, prompting the industry to develop specialized chips optimized for specific tasks like machine learning.

### **2\. The GPU was originally designed for graphics processing; how did it become widely used for machine learning tasks?**

Answer: The GPU was initially a professional graphics processor designed to support massive parallel processing. In the early 21st century, Nvidia pushed for its shift toward general-purpose computing. The key was the release of Nvidia's proprietary CUDA platform, which allowed programmers to directly access the GPU's virtual instruction set and parallel computing units. This enabled the creation of calculation kernels (clusters of collaborating processors) specialized in specific tasks. This approach leveraged the existing mature technical ecosystem, making the GPU highly compatible with current systems and programming languages, which significantly promoted its adoption in machine learning.

### **3\. What unique aspects of the Tensor Processing Unit (TPU) design make it more flexible and efficient than the GPU?**

Answer: The TPU has two unique design features:

1. Computational Precision: The TPU adopts slightly lower computational precision (e.g., using 8-bit integers) to handle different types of workloads. This is based on the insight that neural network predictions often do not require the precision of 32-bit or even 16-bit floating-point numbers. In contrast, GPUs were originally designed for graphics rendering, which relies on the precise relationship between pixels.

2. Systolic Array Architecture: The TPU employs a systolic array architecture, building a processor network where each node executes a portion of the calculation task and then passes the result to the next node until the entire process is complete. This structure allows the TPU to focus on tensor operations with higher speed and energy efficiency.

### **4\. What is the main positioning and operating principle of the NPU? How does it primarily differ from the GPU/TPU?**

Answer:

● Positioning: The NPU (Neural Processing Unit) typically refers to a processor designed specifically for mobile devices, though it is sometimes used as a catch-all term for all specialized AI processors.

● Operating Principle: The NPU operates based on the principle of synaptic weights, a concept borrowed from biology. AI algorithms learn by continuously tracking potential possibilities and strengthening the connection weights of relevant nodes. This is highly significant for power control.

● Key Difference: The NPU is highly customized for AI-enabled devices (like smartphones and IoT devices) and is focused on low power consumption and high integration, contrasting with GPUs used for AI research and training in data centers and TPUs exclusive to Google Cloud infrastructure.

### **5\. What are the differences between ASIC and FPGA, and what AI scenarios are they suitable for?**

Answer: ASIC and FPGA represent two different approaches to chip manufacturing:

3. ASIC (Application-Specific Integrated Circuit): A hard-wired chip customized for specific software. CPUs, GPUs, and TPUs generally operate based on ASICs. They require mass production to be profitable and have long production cycles.

4. FPGA (Field-Programmable Gate Array): A reconfigurable "blank chip." It offers high flexibility, allowing developers to achieve multi-functionality by reprogramming on the same chip.

In AI scenarios:

● ASIC (GPU/TPU): Suitable for scenarios where the technology is mature, large-scale deployment is needed, and extreme performance is required (e.g., large-scale AI training and inference in data centers).

● FPGA: Suitable for AI applications (especially deep learning algorithms) where technology iterates rapidly and frequent testing and iteration are necessary, as it can quickly complete function reconfiguration.
