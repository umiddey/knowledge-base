Chapter 1

What is a bit?

A bit is the smallest useful piece of digital information. Physically, it usually means a signal is being interpreted as low or high, not as some mystical math symbol.

signal level

Digital does not mean abstract

A chip reads voltage ranges and decides whether they count as 0 or 1. That decision is what lets physical circuits behave like logic.

Low

We call the signal 0 when the voltage is below a threshold.

High

We call the signal 1 when the voltage is above another threshold.

Noise margin

The chip has some tolerance so tiny electrical noise does not change the meaning.

The important thing is that a bit is not a little number floating in space. It is an interpreted electrical state.

Binary as voltage

The same wire can mean 0 or 1 depending on whether the signal is near ground or near the supply voltage.

Chapter 2

What is a transistor?

A MOS transistor is a three-terminal device: gate, source, and drain. Voltage on the gate creates or removes a conductive channel between source and drain, which is why digital logic can treat it like a controllable switch.

switch

MOSFET behavior, not just metaphor

The gate is insulated from the channel. It does not carry the main current. Instead, gate voltage changes the electric field in the silicon and decides whether current can flow between source and drain.

Gate

The control terminal. If gate voltage crosses threshold, the device turns on strongly enough to be useful.

Source and drain

The path where current flows when the channel exists. In digital CMOS, we mostly care whether that path looks like a connection or an open circuit.

NMOS vs PMOS

NMOS is good at pulling a node down toward ground. PMOS is good at pulling a node up toward VDD. CMOS uses both.

Threshold voltageA transistor does not turn from perfect off to perfect on instantly. Around threshold, it is in the analog transition region, which is why delay and power matter.
Why switching costs powerCharging and discharging capacitance on wires and gates burns dynamic energy. Static CMOS is efficient mainly because one side is usually off in steady state.
The crucial low-level picture is this: logic values come from transistor networks steering a node either to VDD or to ground.

Closed versus open

This picture is a simplification, but the simplification is useful: gate voltage determines whether source and drain are effectively disconnected or conducting.

Chapter 3

How CMOS makes real logic gates

Digital gates are built from a pull-up network made of PMOS transistors and a pull-down network made of NMOS transistors. One network should pull high while the other stays off.

CMOS

Truth tables come from transistor networks

The lowest useful CMOS gate to memorize is the inverter. Input low turns PMOS on and NMOS off, so output rises to VDD. Input high does the opposite, so output falls to ground.

INVPMOS pull-up, NMOS pull-down
NANDseries NMOS, parallel PMOS
NORparallel NMOS, series PMOS
AND/ORusually built from NAND/NOR plus inversion
NAND matters mostReal standard-cell libraries lean heavily on NAND, NOR, and inverter cells because they are compact and map well to CMOS transistor stacks.
Why delay happensThe output node has capacitance. A transistor network must charge or discharge that capacitance through finite resistance, so every gate has propagation delay.
If you only memorize one deep idea here, make it this: a logic gate is a controlled path to VDD or GND, not an abstract black box.

Truth table as picture

The table is the behavior. The inverter and NAND structures are the mechanism that forces that behavior in silicon.

Chapter 4

How memory starts: latch and feedback

Logic gates compute, but they do not remember. Feedback turns a circuit into storage. That is the jump from pure combinational logic into state.

memory

Why memory needs feedback

If a circuit can feed its output back into its input, it can keep a value instead of forgetting it the moment the input changes.

Latch

A level-sensitive storage element. It can be open or closed depending on an enable signal.

Feedback

The output is routed back so the circuit can reinforce its own state.

Bistability

The whole trick is that the circuit has two stable resting points. It settles into one of them and stays there until a strong enough input kicks it out.

This is the point where “logic” becomes “state.” Once you have bistable storage, you can build registers, counters, pipelines, and eventually whole processors.

Cross-coupled idea

Two gates can watch each other and keep one remembered value alive.

Chapter 4b

SR latch: the first real memory cell

If you see `S` and `R` with outputs `Q` and `Q̅` and no clock, that is a latch. The feedback makes it remember a bit.

latch

What S and R mean

`S` means set the bit to 1. `R` means reset the bit to 0. The latch keeps the result after the input changes away.

S

Set. Push the stored bit toward 1.

R

Reset. Push the stored bit toward 0.

Q / Q̅

One output is the stored bit, the other is its opposite.

Forbidden conditionFor a NOR-based SR latch, asserting both S and R at the same time breaks the intended complement relationship between Q and Q̅. The release can be unpredictable.
Why this matters laterSetup/hold problems in modern synchronous logic are dangerous for the same reason: storage elements can be pushed into ambiguous or metastable behavior.
This is memory without a clock. The dangerous part is not just storing a bit. It is what happens when competing inputs try to change state at nearly the same time.

Cross-coupled latch

Two gates watch each other. That feedback is the whole trick.

Chapter 5

Flip-flops and the clock

A flip-flop is memory that only changes at a clock edge. That edge is what gives digital systems their rhythm.

clocked state

Why the clock matters

The clock makes state changes predictable. Instead of changing whenever the input wiggles, the circuit waits for the right moment.

Edge-triggered

The flip-flop samples its input only on a rising or falling clock edge.

Register

A group of flip-flops storing several bits side by side.

State machine

Memory plus rules for how that memory changes over time.

Setup and holdThe input must be stable for a small window before and after the active edge. Violate that window and the flop may not capture a clean 0 or 1.
MetastabilityIf the input changes too close to the clock edge, the storage node can hover in an analog in-between state before eventually resolving. Synchronizers exist because of this.
This is the point where a circuit starts behaving like a machine that has a past and a future, but only if timing is respected.
Chapter 6

RTL: describing hardware in code

RTL is not software. It is a structured description of wires, gates, and state that synthesis tools can turn into real circuits.

HDL

What Verilog is actually doing

`assign` and `always_comb` describe combinational logic. `always_ff` describes state sampled on a clock edge. Synthesis infers muxes, adders, comparators, and flip-flops from those descriptions.

Combinational

Output depends only on the current inputs. Missing cases in a combinational block can accidentally infer latches.

Sequential

Output depends on current inputs plus stored state. Non-blocking assignment is used so all registers update together at the edge.

Synthesis

The tool reads intent and builds structures: an `if` may become a mux, `+` may become an adder, and an edge-triggered block becomes flip-flops.

Blocking vs non-blocking`=` acts immediately in simulation order. `<=` schedules the update for the end of the time step, which matches how banked registers behave at a clock edge.
Timing still existsRTL can look correct functionally and still fail later because a combinational path is too long to fit inside one clock period.
The big mindset shift is that you are describing a circuit, not writing an ordered list of steps for a CPU to execute.

Code becomes structure

The same idea can be drawn as text and as a circuit.

Chapter 7

FPGA versus ASIC

An FPGA is reconfigurable hardware built from programmable logic blocks. An ASIC is custom silicon built from fixed transistors and gates.

implementation

Same logic, different physical form

An FPGA uses lookup tables and flip-flops to imitate logic. An ASIC builds the logic directly in silicon.

FPGA

Fast to change, slower and less efficient, great for learning and prototyping.

ASIC

Slow and expensive to make, but fast, compact, and power efficient once built.

If you understand LUTs, flip-flops, and gates, you understand why FPGA is a bridge and ASIC is the final physical product.

LUT versus gate

An FPGA can implement logic by looking up outputs in tiny programmable memory. An ASIC wires the answer in directly.

Chapter 8

The physical design flow

Once RTL exists, synthesis maps it to gates, then place and route turns the gates into a physical layout the foundry can fabricate.

RTL to GDSII

From idea to layout

The real chain is longer than the cartoon: write RTL, verify it, synthesize it, insert DFT, floorplan the die, build the power network, place cells, build the clock tree, route wires, run timing, then run physical verification and export GDSII.

Verification first

Simulation, assertions, and formal checks happen before physical work because a beautiful wrong chip is still wrong.

Synthesis and DFT

RTL maps into technology cells, then scan and other test structures are inserted so the finished die is manufacturable.

Floorplan, power, CTS

Macro placement, power grid quality, and clock skew shape whether the design can ever close timing.

STA and signoff

Static timing, DRC, LVS, ERC, IR-drop, and antenna checks decide whether the layout is safe to tape out.

Chapter 9

Why DFT exists

Once chips are manufactured, they need to be tested. DFT makes the circuit controllable and observable enough to find defects economically.

scan chains

Scan chains turn state into a testable shift register

If you can shift values into flip-flops and shift them back out, you can inspect what is otherwise hidden inside the chip.

Controllability

Force internal nodes into a known state.

Observability

Read the state back out after a test cycle.

Chapter 10

Only now: big AI hardware

Once the low-level parts are clear, the larger machines become easier to understand. GPUs, TPUs, and accelerators are just different ways of arranging the same primitives.

AI hardware

Same building blocks, different emphasis

The point is not that GPUs and TPUs are magic. The point is that they optimize different layers of the same stack: MAC arrays, register files, SRAM slices, on-chip interconnect, HBM bandwidth, and compiler-controlled dataflow.

GPU

Many programmable cores, large register files, shared memory, caches, and tensor cores. Excellent when workloads are varied and software flexibility matters.

TPU

A systolic matrix engine with simpler control and a more rigid dataflow. Great when the compiler can feed the array efficiently.

Compiler

Turns a graph into a legal schedule: which tensors live in SRAM, which tiles stream through MACs, and when data moves to and from HBM.

Real compute primitiveA modern AI chip is full of multiply-accumulate units. Everything above the transistor level is organized around keeping those MACs busy.
Real bottleneckMoving operands often costs more energy than multiplying them, so memory hierarchy and dataflow are often more important than raw arithmetic count.
If the low-level primitives are clear, the big picture stops being mystical. It becomes a design choice about arithmetic density, memory movement, and compiler control.

Bottom to top map

The chain is simple, but one extra bridge matters: gates become MACs and SRAM blocks before they become useful AI hardware.

What this deck is trying to teach

AI silicon is not mysterious once the primitives are clear. It is just a chain of physical ideas: voltage becomes bits, bits become transistor networks, those networks become gates, gates become state, state becomes datapaths, and datapaths become chips.

The durable mental model is this: every glamorous AI accelerator is still constrained by transistor physics, clocking discipline, memory movement, and verification reality.
