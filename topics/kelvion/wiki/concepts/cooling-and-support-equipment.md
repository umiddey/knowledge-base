# Cooling and Support Equipment

Beyond its headline heat exchangers, Kelvion sells a set of cooling products that support rotating equipment, transformers, and air-handling systems. The main families are coils, transformer-cooling systems, and engine/generator coolers. These products look narrower than the flagship plate or process lines, but they are important because they sit inside critical infrastructure: power grids, data centers, industrial plants, marine systems, and HVAC equipment.

The coils page gives the best sense of the engineering approach. Kelvion says its fin-and-tube products use different tube diameters, patterns, fin profiles, and materials to fit applications from air heaters to condensers and evaporators. It also claims `8mm` tubes with a patented fin profile can deliver the same or higher performance than larger-tube systems while cutting weight, air-pressure loss by about `20%`, and refrigerant charge by up to `40%`. Transformer cooling systems follow the same philosophy: an oil-air cooler can be mounted directly on the tank, and the premium series has more than `80 years` of manufacturing history.

Engine and generator coolers are the third piece. The exhaust-gas heat exchanger adds a waste-heat-recovery angle, using high-alloy stainless steel to handle temperatures up to `550°C` and reclaim heat for central heating, furnace preheating, coal drying, or Rankine-cycle electricity generation. In practice, this group is the connective tissue between Kelvion’s thermal portfolio and the assets that cannot afford overheating.

## Key Points
- Coils, transformer coolers, and engine/generator coolers are support products for critical infrastructure.
- Kelvion claims 8 mm coil technology can cut air pressure loss by about 20% and refrigerant charge by up to 40%.
- Transformer oil-air cooling has an 80-year manufacturing lineage and prioritizes oil leak prevention.
- Exhaust-gas heat exchangers turn waste heat into useful thermal or electrical output.

## Example
A grid substation can use a transformer oil-air cooler to keep the transformer within its operating window, while a nearby CHP plant uses an exhaust-gas heat exchanger to capture waste heat for district heating. The two products are different, but the logic is the same: preserve asset life and recover energy wherever possible.
