# Heat Rejection and Recovery

This part of Kelvion’s portfolio handles the unglamorous but essential job of dumping heat, reclaiming heat, or doing both at once. The umbrella includes air fin coolers, air-cooled condensers, adiabatic systems, cascade cooling, dry coolers, and related dry/wet hybrid systems. These products are optimized for industrial environments where energy efficiency, water consumption, ambient conditions, and plot-plan constraints all interact.

The technical differences are meaningful. Air fin coolers let customers choose between aluminium and steel fins; Kelvion says steel versions can last up to `30 years` and resist hail and mechanical stress, while aluminium offers lower weight and easier cleaning. Air-cooled condensers are designed around API `661` / ISO `13706` and ASME VIII requirements, with variants such as single-row and A-tube systems. Adiabatic systems can cool the air by about `10°C` through evaporation, and cascade cooling combines dry and wet modes to balance energy, water, and capacity with claimed approaches down to `2K` above wet bulb and up to `100%` adiabatic efficiency.

This portfolio matters because Kelvion often sells the operational outcome rather than the component: lower OPEX, lower water use, or higher availability under load. The data-center and power-sector case studies show that the same thermal logic can be optimized for a dry-cooler installation, a wet season hybrid, or a high-density liquid-cooling deployment.

## Key Points
- Heat rejection is about balancing efficiency, water use, and capacity across seasons.
- Air fin coolers, air-cooled condensers, cascade cooling, and adiabatic systems solve different operational constraints.
- Kelvion emphasizes long-life steel, easy-clean aluminium, and hybrid dry/wet operating modes.
- The portfolio is directly tied to data centres, heat pumps, district energy, and industrial cooling.

## Example
A hyperscale data center in a water-stressed region may use cascade cooling so the system runs in dry mode most of the year, switches to wet mode only when summer conditions spike, and stays within water-use limits without sacrificing uptime. The same site might choose a dry cooler or an adiabatic system depending on whether the design constraint is footprint, water quality, or peak ambient temperature.
