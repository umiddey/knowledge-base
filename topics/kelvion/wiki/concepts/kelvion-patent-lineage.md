# Kelvion Patent Lineage

Kelvion’s public product pages are supported by a patent and prior-art trail that explains why its designs look the way they do. Google Patents shows an asymmetric plate heat exchanger patent, a plate-type exchanger with single-walled and double-walled transfer plates, a generic heat exchanger patent focused on surface structure and ribs, and a heat exchanger patent for perforated fins and tube rows. That mix maps neatly onto Kelvion’s product families in plate, shell-and-tube, and air-fin technologies.

The value of the patent trail is not just legal protection. It documents the design logic behind the products: asymmetry for different pressure drops and fluids, dual walls for safety, ribbing and surface structure for transfer enhancement, and fin perforation for air-side performance. In practice, those ideas appear again in Kelvion’s K°Flex, shell-and-tube, double-safety, air-fin cooler, and coil pages.

This lineage also helps explain the company’s innovation posture. The technical features on the web site are not marketing fluff detached from engineering history; they are the public-facing expression of a long incremental design program that has been formalized in patents, research collaborations, and industrial product families.

## Key Points
- Kelvion’s patents cover plate asymmetry, double-walled transfer plates, surface structure, and fin geometry.
- The patent trail maps directly to current product families.
- The technical emphasis is on transfer enhancement, safety, and compactness.
- Research collaborations like Fraunhofer fit into the same continuity.

## Example
If you compare `K°Flex` to the patent for an asymmetric plate heat exchanger, the design logic is the same: one side is optimized for high flow and low pressure drop, the other for turbulence and transfer. Likewise, a double-safety shell-and-tube exchanger echoes the double-walled transfer-plate patent logic: if one barrier fails, the second barrier preserves containment and gives operators time to react.
