# Process Heat Transfer

Kelvion’s process-heat-transfer portfolio is the severe-duty side of its thermal business. It includes shell-and-tube exchangers, diffusion-bonded units, desublimators, and the `K°Flex` family. The common thread is not simply moving heat, but moving it in environments where pressure, temperature, phase change, leakage risk, and available footprint all matter at once.

The shell-and-tube single family spans everything from modular, mass-produced units to customized industrial builds. Kelvion says its compact fin technology can reduce size by up to `75%` relative to plain-tube designs in oil and air cooling. The double-safety version adds dual-wall tubing and leak detection, with more than `40 years` of experience behind the product line. `K°Flex` pushes into asymmetric plate territory, with operation up to `100 bar` and temperatures from `-200°C` to `550°C`, while `K°Bond` goes much further: up to `1,050 bar`, `-200°C` to `600°C`, and a temperature approach of about `2°C`.

Desublimators are the outlier but an important one. Kelvion says it has delivered more than `1,600` units worldwide since the technology’s invention in `1954`, and that the system is used for material separation through phase change in chemical, pharmaceutical, LNG, and gas-treatment contexts. This is one of the clearest examples of Kelvion spanning from conventional heat exchange to specialized separation engineering.

## Key Points
- Shell-and-tube remains the workhorse for rugged industrial heat transfer.
- Double-safety designs add leak monitoring and media separation for hazardous duty.
- `K°Flex` and `K°Bond` handle extreme pressure, temperature, and footprint constraints.
- Desublimators turn a heat-transfer problem into a phase-separation process.

## Example
An LNG or hydrogen project with little available space may prefer `K°Bond` over a larger shell-and-tube exchanger because diffusion bonding delivers leakage-free service in a package up to six times smaller than conventional shell-and-tube. A chemical plant with toxic media, by contrast, may choose double-safety shell-and-tube because the leak-detection space is a stronger operational safety feature than maximum compactness.
